    How to read this
    • Upside is the headline number, but the scenario range matters more. If Bull and Bear differ by >100% (e.g. Bull says $400, Bear says $150), the DCF itself is highly uncertain — treat the weighted intrinsic as a midpoint guess, not a target.
    • Margin of Safety = (intrinsic − current) / intrinsic. >25% = real value setup; 0% to 25% = mildly cheap; <0% = current price exceeds even the bear case. Buy with conviction only when MoS > 25%.
    • Sensitivity heatmap tells you which assumption your DCF rests on. If the green and red corners are far apart in price, a small change in discount rate or terminal growth flips the verdict — fragile DCF. If colors are similar across the grid — robust DCF.
    • If the "DCF reliability low" warning shows — the underlying free cash flow history is erratic. Switch to the Peers tab and use relative-value multiples instead of trusting the absolute intrinsic number.
    • Decision rule: DCF should corroborate the quant view, not override it. If quant says Buy and DCF says +30%, take the trade. If quant says Buy and DCF says -50%, you're paying for momentum — size accordingly.

    How to read this
    • Overall risk label sets your position size, not your direction. High/Extreme + bullish thesis = take the trade smaller, not skip it. Low + bullish = trade it confidently.
    • Beta vs SPY: >1.3 = amplified market moves both ways (volatile); 0.8-1.3 = market-like; <0.8 = defensive characteristics. High-beta names should never be sized like low-beta names — same conviction, different dollar exposure.
    • Stress scenarios are the single best forward-looking risk number. If 2008 + 2020 + 2022 estimated impact average is <-40%, this is a high-vol name that will give back gains in any real selloff. Plan for it.
    • Always use Half-Kelly, never Full Kelly. Full Kelly assumes your win rate estimate is exact — it never is. Half-Kelly cuts the risk of ruin in half while keeping ~75% of the geometric growth.
    • Macro correlations: SPY corr >0.7 means this is essentially a long-the-market trade with extra noise — your edge needs to be real to justify the single-name risk vs. just buying SPY. Strong negative TLT correlation = your trade is short rates as much as long the company.
    • Decision rule: only size up when conviction is high and risk is moderate or low. High conviction + extreme risk = small position. Low conviction + low risk = small position. Both must align for big size.

    How to read this
    • Relative-value score is the headline. >60 = cheap vs. peers; 40-60 = in line; <40 = expensive. Always ask why — cheap names often deserve to be cheap.
    • For tech/growth names, focus on revenue growth and gross margin. A stock that trades at a premium multiple but has the best growth and margins in the group is justified, not expensive.
    • For mature/value names, focus on P/E, EV/EBITDA, and FCF yield. The cheapest multiple in the group with stable margins is your value signal.
    • The value-trap test: if the target is cheap on multiples and losing on growth/margins, the discount is justified. Buy cheap names that are winning, not declining.
    • Decision rule: use this tab to corroborate or challenge the DCF. If both the DCF and the peer table say "cheap" — high conviction long. If one says cheap and the other expensive — wait.

    How to read this
    • Sentiment score is on a -100 to +100 scale, time-weighted with a 48-hour half-life. >+15 = meaningfully bullish news flow; -15 to +15 = noise; <-15 = bearish narrative.
    • Tape vs. news alignment is the most important field. "Aligned" = news and price agree (confirms the trend). "Conflicted" = news and price disagree — this is the high-information state: the market knows something the news hasn't priced, or the news is wrong.
    • Conflicted + bearish news + rising price = the tape is shrugging off bad news. Often a contrarian buy signal.
    • Conflicted + bullish news + falling price = the tape is rejecting good news. Usually a sell signal — institutional sellers are stepping out.
    • Headline count below 5 = thin coverage. Score is noisier than usual; weight it less in your decision.
    • Trend chart matters more than the snapshot. A score of +30 today after being -20 last week is improving narrative — different from +30 sustained for a month (already-priced-in).
    • Decision rule: sentiment is a tilt, not a thesis. Use it to size up or down a trade you'd already take based on quant + valuation. Never trade on sentiment alone.
